Data Security: Eight Tips to Help Secure Your Office Network

When it comes to securing data, strong protected passwords are the place to start. This helps to prevent hackers and other malicious entities from gaining entry into your system. Another way to help prevent your data from falling into the wrong hands is to secure your physical office network. To this end, there are several steps which make it much more difficult for hackers to gain access to your files or hijack your online accounts through your office network.

1. Encrypt Your Network

Wireless routers and access points don't come secured by default. If you don't enable encryption, anyone can easily connect to your Wi-Fi network. To prevent this, you should use at a minimum the available WPA2 security level. Use a strong encryption password of at least 63 characters including upper and lower case letters, numbers and special symbols.

If you have employees, you should use the Enterprise (EAP) mode of WPA2 security. This prevents employees from being able to access your encryption password, and prevents the password from being stored on computers and devices in the event they are lost or stolen. This allows access to your Wi-Fi network to be based on specified individual usernames and passwords given to each user. Thus, if an employee leaves your practice, you can quickly and easily revoke access for that particular user instead of changing the encryption password on all of your equipment.

2. Hide Your Network

Most wireless access points (APs) and routers automatically transmit their network name (SSID) into open air at regular intervals (every few seconds). This feature of Wi-Fi network protocols is intended to allow clients to dynamically discover and roam between WLANs.

However, this feature also makes it easier for hackers to break into your office network. Because SSIDs are not encrypted or otherwise scrambled, it becomes easy to grab one by snooping the WLAN looking for SSID broadcast messages coming from a router or access point. Knowing your SSID brings hackers one step closer to a successful intrusion.

In an office Wi-Fi network, roaming is largely unnecessary and the SSID broadcast feature serves no useful purpose. You should disable SSID broadcasting to improve the security of your network. Once your wireless clients are manually configured with the right SSID, they no longer require these broadcast messages.

Note that disabling SSID broadcast is just one of many techniques for tightening security on a Wi-Fi network. This technique is not 100% effective, as hackers can still detect the SSID by sniffing different messages in the Wi-Fi protocol. Still, using techniques like SSID broadcast disable makes it more likely that would-be intruders will bypass an office network seeking easier targets elsewhere.

3. Secure Your Equipment

Make sure that all of your network equipment, including routers and access points, is hidden and secured from the public, visitors and employees who do not require access. If equipment is accessible, an intruder may be able to simply reset your wireless router which could remove the encryption, allowing easy access to your system.

Also make sure there aren't any Ethernet wall ports available to the public or visitors. An intruder could potentially simply plug in and access your network. Wall ports should always be in areas inaccessible to the public and non-authorized personnel.

4. Limit Access to Your Shared Folders

Any shared resources, including shared folders, on your network should be secured by setting file and sharing permissions and defining exactly who has access and the type of access granted to each person.

5. Create a Separate Environment for Your Guests

You should never let the public or guests onto your private business network. Even after shared resources are secured, guests on your private network still might be able to eavesdrop on your Internet traffic and capture or hijack online accounts that you are logged into. If you want to offer guest access, assign them a separate account, or even better, consider providing an entirely separate Internet connection through a separate router.

6. Use Filtering for Your Approved MAC Addresses

Though MAC Address filtering can easily be circumvented by hackers, this approach can help deter snoopers and make it more difficult and time consuming for a hacker to gain access to your network. This may prompt a hacker to move along in search of easier targets. You determine which computers and devices that you wish to allow to access your network, and you identify the computers by their unique MAC address. Computers that are not listed on the "white-list" of approved MAC addresses will not be allowed access to your network.

7. Make Sure Your Firewall is Installed and Functioning

A firewall provides an increased level of security and should be implemented on your router and all computers that access the Internet.

8. Choose Your Router Location Carefully

Wireless signals can transmit up to several hundred feet, so the signal from your network could potentially be broadcast outside of your office. You can help limit the area that your wireless signal reaches and is visible by positioning your router or access point near the center of your office rather than near an outside office wall or window. This has the added benefit of providing better coverage and accessibility throughout your office.
